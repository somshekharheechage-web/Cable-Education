ARMORING MACHINE

Applying steel wire or steel tape armour over the cable core assembly — providing mechanical protection against impact, crush, and rodent attack for buried and industrial cables.

// OVERVIEW

WHAT IS CABLE ARMORING?

Armoring applies a layer of steel wire or steel tape over the laid-up cable assembly to provide mechanical protection against impact, crush forces, rodent attack, and the demands of direct burial or industrial installation. Armoured cables are specified wherever physical damage cannot be prevented by the jacket alone.

The armoring machine applies armour wires or tapes helically over the bedded assembly. After armoring, the cable moves to the jacketing extruder for the final outer sheath.

// ARMOUR TYPES

TYPES OF CABLE ARMOUR

🔩
Steel Wire Armour (SWA)
Individual galvanised steel wires wound helically around the cable. The most common armour type for power cables. Wire diameter is typically 0.8–3.15 mm. Provides excellent crush and impact resistance. Specified in IEC 60502.
📜
Steel Tape Armour (STA)
Two layers of thin steel tape (0.2–0.5 mm) wound helically in opposing directions. More compact than SWA with better crush resistance. Suitable for cables in stable soil conditions and short runs with limited installation tension.
🌀
Aluminium Wire Armour (AWA)
Used on single-core cables where steel wire would cause inductive heating from the cable's magnetic field. Aluminium does not form a closed magnetic circuit, eliminating eddy current losses. Essential for single-core HV cables.
⚡
Double Steel Wire Armour (DSWA)
Two layers of steel wire in opposing helical directions. Used for submarine cables and extreme mechanical stress installations — provides tensile strength to handle deep-water installation tensions and seabed movement.

// PROCESS

ARMORING MACHINE OPERATION

1
Wire Pay-off Cage
Steel wires are fed from a rotating cage carrying multiple supply bobbins arranged around the cable path. All positions must maintain equal, controlled tension to ensure uniform lay angle and wire spacing around the circumference.
2
Forming Die
Wires converge through a closing die that positions each wire at the correct lay angle. Die diameter is set to give the target coverage and wire-to-wire gap. Wires must sit tangentially on the bedding surface without crossing or lifting.
3
Lay Length Control
Lay length = haul-off speed divided by cage rotation speed. Short lay gives more wires per metre and higher crush resistance; long lay reduces material usage. Typical lay: 10–20× the armoured cable OD. Specified in the cable construction drawing.
4
Armour Continuity Test
After armoring, the steel wire or tape is tested for electrical continuity across the full drum length. For SWA cables, the armour is the fault return path in the electrical installation — any broken wire would compromise protection and must be detected here.
