BUNCHER
MACHINE

Twisting multiple drawn wires together into a single stranded conductor — the process that gives cables their flexibility, strength, and electrical performance.

// OVERVIEW

WHAT IS A BUNCHER?

A buncher (also called a stranding machine or cable bunching machine) twists multiple individual wires together into a single conductor bundle. This stranded construction is far more flexible than a single solid wire of the same cross-section, making it suitable for cables that must be bent, flexed, and routed around obstacles during installation.

The key parameter is the lay length — the axial distance for one complete twist of the bundle. Shorter lay length means more twists per metre, greater flexibility, and better current sharing between strands, but also higher material usage and reduced production speed.

// BUNCHING TYPES

BUNCHING TECHNIQUES

⭕
Concentric Stranding
Wires arranged in concentric layers around a central core. Each layer has a defined number of wires (1+6+12+18…). Alternating lay direction between layers gives a compact, round, and dimensionally stable conductor. Standard for power cables and medium/high voltage applications.
〰️
Regular (Parallel) Bunching
All wires twisted together without a defined layered structure. Simpler and faster than concentric stranding. Used where flexibility is the priority — flexible cords, welding cables, and portable power cables. Conductor is rounder and softer than concentric.
🪢
Rope Stranding
Multiple bunched sub-conductors are twisted together into a larger rope-lay conductor. Provides maximum flexibility for very large cross-sections. Used in welding leads, submarine cables, and flexible busbar applications where conductor size exceeds ~150 mm².
🔄
Planetary Bunching
Bobbins rotate around a central axis (planetary motion) as the cable is pulled through. Enables very high-speed production — up to 3,000 RPM on fine wire machines. Each bobbin maintains correct back-tension via its own brake system.

// KEY PARAMETERS

PROCESS PARAMETERS

1
Lay Length
The axial distance for one complete 360° twist. Shorter lay = more flexible, better electrical performance, higher material usage. Typical range: 8×–20× the conductor diameter. IEC 60228 specifies maximum lay lengths for each conductor class.
2
Lay Direction
Right-hand (Z) or left-hand (S) twist. For multi-layer conductors, alternating lay directions between layers prevents the cable from unravelling and improves flexibility. The outer layer direction is always specified on the cable drawing.
3
Back Tension
Each bobbin supply must maintain consistent back-tension on its wire. Uneven tension causes one strand to be under compression while others are in excess tension — leading to birdcaging, conductor ovality, or strand breakage at the die.
4
Compaction
After bunching, the conductor may be passed through a compacting die to reduce its outer diameter, improve roundness, and smooth the surface before insulation extrusion. Compacted conductors give thinner insulation walls for the same conductor OD.
