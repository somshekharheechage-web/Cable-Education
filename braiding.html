BRAIDING MACHINE

Weaving metallic or non-metallic strands over an insulated cable core to form a shielding layer — protecting against EMI and providing mechanical reinforcement.

// OVERVIEW

WHAT IS CABLE BRAIDING?

A braiding machine interlaces strands of wire or yarn over a cable core in a diagonal weave pattern — creating a tubular mesh layer. In cable manufacturing, braiding serves two main functions: electromagnetic shielding (using tinned copper or bare copper wire) and mechanical reinforcement (using fibreglass, Kevlar, or stainless steel).

The key performance metric is braid coverage — the percentage of the underlying surface covered by the braid strands. Coverage of 85–95% is typical for shielded cables. Higher coverage means better EMI attenuation but also more material, slower speed, and a stiffer cable.

// MACHINE OPERATION

HOW A BRAIDER WORKS

1
Carriers
Small bobbins (carriers) loaded with wire or yarn are mounted on a circular track in two groups — clockwise and counter-clockwise. They travel around each other, weaving over and under as the cable passes through the centre. A typical braider has 8–144 carriers.
2
Braiding Point
The point where opposing carrier strands cross and lock onto the cable surface. The distance from the carrier track to this point — the braiding angle — determines braid lay and coverage percentage. Steep angles give high coverage; shallow angles give lower coverage at higher speed.
3
Take-up Speed
A caterpillar haul-off pulls the cable at constant speed. The ratio of haul-off speed to carrier rotation speed sets the braid pitch. Increasing line speed reduces coverage; reducing it increases coverage and material usage.

// COVERAGE FORMULA

BRAID COVERAGE FORMULA

C = 1 − (1 − n·d / (π·D·sin θ))²
n = carriers per group  |  d = strand wire diameter (mm)  |  D = cable OD under braid (mm)  |  θ = braid angle (degrees)
