INSULATION

PVC, XLPE, PE, PTFE, EPR, Silicone — insulation materials, dielectric properties, and temperature ratings.

// OVERVIEW
WHAT IS CABLE INSULATION?

Insulation is the electrically non-conductive layer applied over each conductor to prevent current from flowing between conductors or to ground. It must maintain its dielectric properties across the full operating temperature range and resist the mechanical and chemical stresses of the installation environment.

Insulation material choice determines the cable's voltage rating, maximum operating temperature, flexibility, chemical resistance, and flammability classification.

// PROCESS
INSULATION PROCESS NOTES
1
Extrusion
Thermoplastic insulation materials (PVC, PE, XLPE) are applied by extrusion. The conductor passes through the crosshead die while molten polymer is forced over it. Wall thickness and concentricity are controlled by die and tip sizing.
2
Cross-linking (for XLPE)
After extrusion, XLPE is cross-linked by peroxide chemistry (in a steam or dry cure tube) or by irradiation. Cross-linking creates 3D molecular bonds that prevent melting at elevated temperatures — giving XLPE its superior thermal performance over standard PE.
3
Spark Testing
Every metre of insulated conductor is tested with an inline high-voltage spark tester. The electrode voltage is set to 3–5× the rated voltage. Any pinhole or thin spot creates a discharge, triggering an immediate alarm and line stop.
4
Colour Coding
Insulation colour identifies each core. IEC 60446 specifies: Green/Yellow = Earth, Blue = Neutral, Brown/Black/Grey = Live. Cable manufacturers add pigment masterbatch to the extrusion compound to achieve the required colour consistently along the entire drum length.
