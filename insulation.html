INSULATION

PVC, XLPE, PE, EPR, PTFE, silicone — insulation materials, temperature ratings, dielectric properties, and selection.

// OVERVIEW

WHAT IS CABLE INSULATION?

Insulation surrounds each conductor to prevent current leakage, maintain electrical isolation between conductors, and protect against environmental hazards. The insulation material determines the cable's maximum operating voltage, temperature rating, dielectric loss, and chemical resistance.

Selecting the wrong insulation compound is one of the most common causes of premature cable failure. Every insulation material involves trade-offs between cost, electrical performance, mechanical strength, and temperature range.

// MATERIAL GUIDE

INSULATION MATERIALS

1
PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)
Max temp 70°C. The most widely used insulation due to low cost, good processability, and inherent flame retardancy. Good moisture and abrasion resistance. Limitations: releases HCl gas in fire, poor low-temperature flexibility below −5°C, higher dielectric loss than PE. Used in building wire, control cables, and general-purpose wiring.
2
XLPE (Cross-Linked Polyethylene)
Max temp 90°C conductor (250°C short circuit). Cross-linking converts thermoplastic PE into a thermoset — dramatically improving thermal and mechanical performance. Low dielectric loss, excellent voltage withstand, and good chemical resistance. Industry standard for medium and high voltage power cables per IEC 60502 and IEC 60840.
3
Polyethylene (PE / MDPE / HDPE)
Max temp 75°C. Excellent dielectric properties — low εᵣ (2.3) and very low loss tangent. The standard insulation for coaxial cables, Ethernet, and telecommunications. Resistant to moisture and most chemicals. Does not support combustion without an additive. HDPE is stiffer; MDPE balances flexibility and stiffness for outdoor cables.
4
Foam PE (FPE / Cellular PE)
PE with controlled air cells — typically 40–65% foam content. Lower dielectric constant (1.4–1.8) than solid PE reduces capacitance and improves velocity of propagation. Essential for high-frequency coaxial cables where low signal loss is critical — CATV, broadband, LMR antenna cables. Requires careful extrusion to maintain uniform cell structure.
5
EPR (Ethylene Propylene Rubber)
Max temp 90°C. Outstanding flexibility even at low temperatures, excellent ozone and UV resistance, and good electrical properties in wet conditions. More expensive than XLPE but preferred for mining cables, shipboard wiring, and portable power cables that must flex continuously in harsh environments.
6
PTFE (Teflon)
Max temp 260°C. Exceptional chemical inertness, ultra-low dielectric constant (2.1), and the widest temperature range of any cable insulation. Used in aerospace wiring, high-frequency microwave cables, chemical plant instrumentation, and military applications. High cost limits use to demanding applications.
7
Silicone Rubber
Max temp 180°C continuously (200°C+ short term). Remains flexible at −60°C. Good electrical properties and excellent resistance to ozone, UV, and weathering. Used for flexible heater cables, oven wiring, automotive engine wiring, and any application combining extreme temperature with required flexibility.
