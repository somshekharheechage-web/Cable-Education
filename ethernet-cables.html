// OVERVIEW

WHAT ARE ETHERNET CABLES?

Ethernet cables are the standard medium for wired local area network (LAN) connections. They allow devices — computers, routers, switches, modems, and servers — to communicate and transfer data reliably over short to medium distances.

Unlike wireless connections, Ethernet cables provide consistent bandwidth, lower latency, and greater security, making them essential in professional, industrial, and data-centre environments.

// PERFORMANCE CATEGORIES

CABLE CATEGORIES

Ethernet cables are categorised by their maximum bandwidth and transmission speed. Higher categories offer greater performance and reduced crosstalk.

Category Max Speed Bandwidth Max Distance Shielding Typical Use
Cat 5e 1 Gbps 100 MHz 100 m UTP / FTP Home & small office networks
Cat 6 10 Gbps 250 MHz 55 m @ 10G UTP / STP Office LANs, demanding applications
Cat 6a 10 Gbps 500 MHz 100 m @ 10G F/UTP, S/FTP Data centres, enterprise networks
Cat 7 10 Gbps 600 MHz 100 m S/FTP (fully shielded) High-interference environments
Cat 8 25–40 Gbps 2000 MHz 30 m S/FTP Data centre top-of-rack connections

// CONSTRUCTION

CABLE CONSTRUCTION

Conductors

Standard Ethernet cables contain 4 twisted pairs (8 wires total) of copper conductors. Each pair is colour-coded for easy identification. Conductors are either solid copper (for fixed installations) or stranded copper (for flexible patch cables).

Insulation

Each conductor is individually insulated, typically with polyethylene (PE) or FEP (for high-temperature or plenum-rated cables). Insulation separates pairs and controls the dielectric constant, which directly affects signal speed.

Shielding

Unshielded (UTP) cables rely on twisting alone to cancel interference. Shielded variants add foil (F/) around individual pairs or a braid (S/) around all pairs to protect against external EMI — essential near motors, power cables, or RF sources.

Connectors

All standard Ethernet cables terminate in RJ45 connectors with 8 gold-plated pins. Wiring follows either T568A or T568B standards. Cat 7 may use GG45 or TERA connectors for full shielding continuity.

// SHIELDING CODES

UNDERSTANDING SHIELDING NOTATION

🔵
UTP — Unshielded Twisted Pair
No overall shield. Relies entirely on pair twisting for noise rejection. Lightest, most flexible, easiest to terminate. Suitable for most office environments.
🟡
F/UTP — Foil over all pairs
A single aluminium foil wraps all four pairs together. Good protection against external EMI without the bulk of braided shielding. Common in Cat 6a.
🟠
S/FTP — Braid + individual foils
Each pair has its own foil shield, plus an overall braided screen. Maximum EMI protection. Used in Cat 7 and Cat 8 for the most demanding environments.
🟢
SF/UTP — Braid + foil overall
Combines foil and braid around all pairs without individual pair shields. A middle ground between F/UTP and S/FTP for moderately noisy environments.

// INSTALLATION

INSTALLATION GUIDELINES

1
Keep runs under 100 m (328 ft)
The IEEE 802.3 standard limits horizontal cable runs to 100 m for Cat 5e through Cat 6a. Beyond this, signal attenuation causes errors. Use a switch or repeater for longer distances.
2
Avoid sharp bends
Maintain a minimum bend radius of 4× the cable diameter. Sharp bends deform the twisted pairs, increase crosstalk, and degrade performance — especially critical for Cat 6a and above.
3
Separate from power cables
Run data cables at least 200 mm away from power cables. Where crossing is unavoidable, cross at 90° to minimise inductive coupling. Use shielded cable in electrically noisy environments.
4
Use the correct category throughout
A link's performance is limited by its lowest-rated component. If you install Cat 6a cable but use Cat 5e patch panels or keystones, the entire link performs to Cat 5e. Match all components.
5
Test after installation
Use a certified cable tester to verify wire map, length, attenuation, NEXT, and return loss. Field certification is required for warranty claims from most cable manufacturers.
