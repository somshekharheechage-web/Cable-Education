STRENGTH MEMBERS

Kevlar, fibreglass, steel wire, polyester, and UHMWPE — tensile strength elements that protect cables during installation and flexing.

// OVERVIEW

WHAT ARE STRENGTH MEMBERS?

Strength members are non-current-carrying elements added to a cable specifically to bear tensile load, resist crush, or limit elongation under mechanical stress. They protect the signal-carrying or power-carrying components from forces that would otherwise stretch, break, or degrade them.

They are critical in three scenarios: installation (pulling tension through conduits or over pulleys), suspended cable (aerial drops, vertical risers), and dynamic flexing (robotics, trailing cables, elevators) where repeated mechanical cycling would fatigue the conductors without reinforcement.

// MATERIAL TYPES

STRENGTH MEMBER MATERIALS

1
Aramid Fibre (Kevlar® / Twaron®)
The most widely used strength member for fibre optic and flexible cables. Tensile strength ~2.8 GPa — approximately 5× stronger than steel by weight. Extremely lightweight, non-conductive, and non-metallic. Does not corrode. Applied as a yarn braid or bundle around the cable core. Cut with scissors (not knife) — shear loading cuts aramid cleanly. Used in fiber optic cables, military cable, and high-flex industrial trailing cables.
2
Fibreglass (GRP — Glass Reinforced Plastic)
Glass fibre yarns or GRP rods provide high tensile strength with very low elongation (important for fibre optic cables where strain on fibres must be minimal). Non-conductive and lightweight. GRP central members are used in outdoor fibre optic cables as the primary anti-buckling and anti-stretch element. Fibre glass yarn is also used in high-temperature cables as heat-resistant reinforcement around silicone-insulated conductors.
3
Steel Wire / Steel Strand
High tensile steel (HTS) wire or strand provides the highest tensile strength per cross-section area. Used in overhead self-supporting cables (ADSS, figure-8), submarine cables requiring installation tension resistance, and elevator travelling cables. Conductive — must be electrically isolated from signal conductors. Heavier than aramid or fibre glass but much cheaper for large cross-sections.
4
Polyester (PET) Yarn
Lower strength than aramid but significantly cheaper. Used as a semi-strength member in patch cables, flexible cords, and data cables where moderate pull resistance is needed without the cost of aramid. Also used as a binding element to hold cable components together during assembly — dual-purpose filler/strength member.
5
UHMWPE (Dyneema® / Spectra®)
Ultra-high molecular weight polyethylene fibre — higher specific strength than aramid, excellent abrasion resistance, and very low weight. Used in the most demanding subsea and aerospace cable applications where weight is critical and installation loads are extreme. More expensive than aramid but increasingly used in deepwater ROV umbilicals and drone tethers.
