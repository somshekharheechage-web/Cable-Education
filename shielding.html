SHIELDING

Foil, braid, spiral, and combination shields — shielding effectiveness, ISO notation, and correct termination practice.

// OVERVIEW

WHY CABLES NEED SHIELDING

Shielding is a conductive layer applied around insulated conductors to attenuate electromagnetic interference (EMI) and radio frequency interference (RFI). Without shielding, cables act as antennas — both radiating interference to nearby equipment and picking up noise from their environment.

Shielding effectiveness (SE) is measured in decibels (dB). A shield with 40 dB SE reduces the electric field to 1% of the unshielded value. The type, coverage, and termination method of the shield all affect SE — even a perfect shield provides no protection if it is poorly terminated at the connector.

// SHIELD TYPES

TYPES OF CABLE SHIELDING

1
Foil Shield (Aluminium or Copper)
A thin metallic foil (typically 0.02–0.05 mm aluminium/polyester laminate) wrapped helically or longitudinally around the core. Provides 100% coverage and excellent high-frequency EMI attenuation. Very low add-on diameter. A drain wire runs inside the foil to provide a solderable ground connection. Most cost-effective shielding for data and instrumentation cables.
2
Braided Shield
Woven mesh of tinned copper wires — typically 85–97% coverage. Superior to foil at low frequencies and provides excellent mechanical protection. More flexible than foil and better at surviving repeated bending. Coverage percentage directly determines shielding effectiveness. Used in audio cables, instrumentation, coaxial cables, and demanding signal applications.
3
Spiral (Serve) Shield
Copper wires wound helically in one direction — like a spring. Provides 95–98% coverage and maximum flexibility, making it ideal for cables that flex continuously (microphone cables, headphone cables, patch cables). However, impedance increases when bent — not suitable for RF or high-frequency signal cables.
4
Combination Shield (Foil + Braid)
Inner foil provides high-frequency coverage and barrier continuity; outer braid adds low-frequency attenuation, mechanical protection, and a robust ground path. The gold standard for demanding environments — industrial instrumentation, medical equipment, and broadcast cables. Typical SE: 80–100 dB across a wide frequency range.
5
Semi-Conductive Tape Screen
Used in medium and high voltage power cables — not for EMI. A carbon-loaded semi-conductive compound applied over the insulation smooths out the electric field at the insulation-screen interface, preventing partial discharge. Essential for cables above 1 kV per IEC 60502.

// KEY RULE

SHIELDING TERMINATION

⚠ A shield is only as good as its termination
An improperly terminated shield can be worse than no shield at all — it can act as an antenna. The shield must be connected to a low-impedance ground at both ends for differential-mode rejection. For single-ended audio, connect at the source end only to prevent ground loops. Pigtail terminations degrade HF performance — always use 360° circumferential clamp terminations above 10 MHz.
