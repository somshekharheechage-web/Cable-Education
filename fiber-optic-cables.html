// OVERVIEW

HOW FIBER OPTIC WORKS

Fiber optic cables transmit data as pulses of light through thin strands of glass or plastic called optical fibers. Light travels through the fiber by a principle called total internal reflection — the fiber core has a higher refractive index than the surrounding cladding, causing light to continuously bounce along the core without escaping.

Because light is the carrier rather than electrical current, fiber is immune to electromagnetic interference, can span vastly greater distances without signal repeaters, and supports far higher bandwidths than any copper medium. It is the foundation of the global internet backbone, long-haul telecommunications, and modern data centres.

// FIBER TYPES

SINGLE-MODE vs MULTI-MODE

Single-Mode Fiber (SMF)
Core diameter: 8–10 µm. Carries a single ray (mode) of light. Near-zero modal dispersion enables transmission distances of 10–100+ km without amplification. Used in telecoms networks, submarine cables, and long-haul backbones. Requires laser light sources. OS1 (tight-buffered indoor) and OS2 (loose-tube outdoor) are the standard grades.

Multi-Mode Fiber (MMF)
Core diameter: 50 µm or 62.5 µm. Carries multiple light modes simultaneously. Modal dispersion limits distance to ~300–550 m for 10G applications, but large core makes coupling to LEDs and VCSELs easy and cost-effective. Ideal for data centre and campus networks. OM1–OM5 grades with OM4/OM5 supporting 40G/100G over 100–150 m.

// CONSTRUCTION

CABLE CONSTRUCTION

1
Core
Ultra-pure silica glass (or plastic for POF). The core carries the light signal. Its refractive index is precisely controlled during manufacturing — even tiny impurities cause signal loss measured in dB/km.
2
Cladding
Surrounds the core with a slightly lower refractive index glass layer (125 µm total diameter). The refractive index difference between core and cladding creates total internal reflection, confining light to the core.
3
Buffer Coating
Acrylate polymer coating (250 µm) applied immediately after drawing. Protects against mechanical damage, moisture, and handling stress without affecting optical performance.
4
Strength Members
Kevlar (aramid) yarns, fibreglass rods, or steel wires carry tensile load. Glass fiber itself is brittle — without strength members, installation pulling forces would shatter the fiber.
5
Outer Jacket
PVC, LSZH, or polyethylene depending on the installation environment. Outdoor cables add a moisture barrier (water-blocking tape or gel) and often a corrugated steel or aluminium armour layer.

// CONNECTORS

COMMON CONNECTORS

🔵
LC (Lucent Connector)
1.25 mm ferrule. The dominant connector in data centres and high-density applications. Small form factor allows twice the port density of SC connectors.
🟡
SC (Subscriber Connector)
2.5 mm ferrule with push-pull latching. Widely used in telecom and CATV. Colour-coded: blue = single-mode, beige/aqua = multi-mode.
🟠
ST (Straight Tip)
2.5 mm bayonet-style connector. Common in legacy multi-mode campus and building installations. Being replaced by LC in new deployments.
🟢
MPO/MTP
Multi-fiber push-on connector carrying 12 or 24 fibers in a single ferrule. Essential for 40G/100G/400G parallel optic applications in hyperscale data centres.
