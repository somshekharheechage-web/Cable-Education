COAXIAL
CABLES

Centre conductor, dielectric, shield, jacket — the classic RF transmission line used in CATV, CCTV, antenna feeds, and high-frequency signal systems.

// OVERVIEW

WHAT IS A COAXIAL CABLE?

A coaxial cable — or "coax" — consists of a central conductor surrounded by a tubular insulating layer, a tubular conducting shield, and an outer insulating jacket. All layers share the same geometric axis, hence the name co-axial.

This concentric design confines the electromagnetic field between the inner conductor and outer shield, dramatically reducing signal radiation and external interference compared to open-wire transmission lines. Coax is the standard choice for RF, video, and broadband signal transmission.

// CONSTRUCTION

LAYER BY LAYER

1
Centre Conductor
Solid or stranded copper, copper-clad aluminium (CCA), or copper-clad steel (CCS). Solid conductors offer lower resistance at high frequencies; stranded conductors provide flexibility. Diameter determines the cable's characteristic impedance in combination with the dielectric.
2
Dielectric Insulation
Typically solid PE, foam PE, or PTFE. The dielectric constant of this layer — along with conductor geometry — determines characteristic impedance (50 Ω or 75 Ω) and velocity of propagation. Foam dielectrics reduce signal loss at high frequencies by lowering the dielectric constant.
3
Outer Conductor / Shield
May be a solid tube (hardline), corrugated tube, foil, braided wire, or a combination. The shield serves as both the return current path and the EMI barrier. Braid coverage percentage (85–98%) directly affects shielding effectiveness. Quad-shield cables use two foils and two braids for maximum isolation.
4
Outer Jacket
PVC for general indoor use, PE or LLDPE for direct burial, LSZH for plenum/riser in safety-critical environments. The jacket protects against moisture, abrasion, UV radiation, and chemicals.

// IMPEDANCE

50 Ω vs 75 Ω

50 Ω — RF & Data
Optimised for power handling and minimum conductor loss. Standard for RF test equipment, transmitters, antenna systems, amateur radio, cellular infrastructure, and Wi-Fi. Never substitute 75 Ω coax in a 50 Ω system — impedance mismatch causes signal reflections and power loss.
75 Ω — Video & CATV
Optimised for minimum signal attenuation over long cable runs. The industry standard for cable television, satellite receivers, CCTV cameras, and broadcast video. RG-6 and RG-11 are the dominant 75 Ω types in residential and commercial installations.
