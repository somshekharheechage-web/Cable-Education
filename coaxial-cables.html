COAXIAL CABLES

Centre conductor, dielectric, outer shield, jacket — the classic RF transmission line for CATV, antenna, and signal systems.

// OVERVIEW
WHAT IS A COAXIAL CABLE?

A coaxial cable consists of a central conductor surrounded by a tubular insulating layer (dielectric), a tubular conducting shield, and an outer jacket. All layers share the same axis — hence co-axial.

The concentric design confines the electromagnetic field between inner and outer conductors, reducing signal radiation and external interference. Coax is standard for RF, video, and broadband signal transmission.

// CONSTRUCTION
LAYER BY LAYER
1
Centre Conductor
Solid or stranded copper, copper-clad aluminium (CCA), or copper-clad steel. Solid conductors offer lower resistance at high frequencies. Diameter determines impedance in combination with the dielectric.
2
Dielectric Insulation
Solid PE, foam PE, or PTFE. The dielectric constant determines characteristic impedance (50Ω or 75Ω) and velocity of propagation. Foam dielectrics reduce signal attenuation at high frequencies.
3
Outer Conductor / Shield
Solid tube (hardline), corrugated tube, foil, braided wire, or combination. The shield is both the return current path and EMI barrier. Braid coverage % (85–98%) determines shielding effectiveness.
4
Outer Jacket
PVC for indoor, PE or LLDPE for burial, LSZH for safety-critical environments. Protects against moisture, abrasion, UV radiation, and chemicals.

// IMPEDANCE
50 Ω vs 75 Ω
50 Ω — RF & Data Systems
Optimised for power handling and minimum conductor loss. Standard for RF test equipment, transmitters, antenna systems, amateur radio, cellular, and Wi-Fi. Never substitute 75 Ω in a 50 Ω system — signal reflections and power loss result.
75 Ω — Video & CATV
Optimised for minimum signal attenuation over long runs. Industry standard for cable TV, satellite, CCTV, and broadcast video. RG-6 and RG-11 are the dominant 75 Ω types in residential and commercial installations.
