EXTRUDER
MACHINE

Melting and forcing polymer compound over a moving conductor — the process that applies insulation, bedding, and jacket layers to a cable.

// OVERVIEW

THE EXTRUSION PROCESS

A cable extruder heats thermoplastic or thermoset polymer compound (PVC, XLPE, PE, LSZH, silicone) and forces it through a crosshead die over the moving conductor. As the material exits the die and cools in a water trough, it solidifies into a uniform insulation or jacket layer.

Extrusion is used at multiple stages: insulation of individual conductors, bedding between cores and armour, and jacketing the outer sheath. Each pass requires precise control of temperature, screw speed, line speed, and die geometry to maintain consistent wall thickness and concentricity.

// MACHINE COMPONENTS

KEY EXTRUDER COMPONENTS

1
Hopper
The material feed point. Granules or pellets of polymer compound are loaded here. A hopper dryer is often fitted above to remove moisture from hygroscopic materials (PVC, nylon, LSZH) — moisture in the melt causes bubbles, voids, and poor surface finish.
2
Barrel & Screw
The barrel is a heated cylinder, typically 20–120 mm diameter, containing the rotating screw. The screw has three zones: Feed (conveys granules inward), Compression (melts and compresses material), and Metering (delivers uniform melt at constant pressure to the die).
3
Temperature Zones
The barrel is divided into 3–6 independent heating zones controlled by PID controllers. Temperature rises from the feed zone (~140°C for PVC) to the metering zone (~190°C). The crosshead and die also have their own independently controlled heating zones.
4
Crosshead Die
The crosshead redirects melt flow 90° onto the moving conductor. Inside are a die tip (which the conductor passes through) and a die (which shapes the outer surface). The gap between tip and die determines the insulation wall thickness and concentricity.
5
Water Trough & Cooling
The extruded cable passes immediately into a water cooling trough — typically 20–60 m long for high-speed lines. Water temperature is staged (warm to cold) to prevent surface crazing. For XLPE, a steam or nitrogen tube vulcanising (CV) tube replaces the open water trough.
6
Spark Tester
An inline high-voltage electrode (typically 5–30 kV AC or DC) tests every centimetre of insulation for pinholes, voids, and thin spots as it leaves the cooling trough. Any fault triggers an alarm and marks the defect location. Mandatory for insulated conductors per IEC 60811.

// DIE TYPES

PRESSURE vs TUBE EXTRUSION

Pressure Extrusion
The die tip is flush with or inside the die orifice. Melt contacts the conductor under pressure inside the die, giving excellent concentricity and adhesion. Used for PVC, LSZH, and PE insulation where tight wall control is critical. The die tip calculator on this site is used for this geometry.
Tube (Vacuum) Extrusion
The die tip protrudes beyond the die. A tube of melt is formed and then drawn down onto the conductor outside the die. A vacuum port collapses the tube onto the conductor. Used for XLPE and applications where the insulation must not be bonded to the conductor screen.

// PROCESS CONTROL

CRITICAL CONTROL PARAMETERS

ParameterEffect if Too HighEffect if Too Low
Melt TemperatureDegradation, discolouration, bubblesPoor fusion, rough surface, high motor load
Screw Speed (RPM)Melt overheating, surgingUnder-feeding, wall thickness variation
Line SpeedThin walls, neckingThick walls, excess material usage
