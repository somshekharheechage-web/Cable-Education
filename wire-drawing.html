WIRE
DRAWING

Reducing wire rod diameter through a series of precision dies — the first and most fundamental step in cable conductor manufacturing.

// OVERVIEW

WHAT IS WIRE DRAWING?

Wire drawing is a metalworking process where a wire rod — typically 8–12 mm diameter copper or aluminium — is pulled through a series of progressively smaller dies to reduce its diameter and increase its length. Each pass through a die is called a draft, and each die reduces the cross-sectional area by a controlled percentage known as the reduction in area (RA).

The process work-hardens the metal, improving tensile strength. After drawing, the wire is often annealed (heat treated) to restore ductility and flexibility, which is essential for cables that must be bent and flexed during installation and use.

// MACHINE TYPES

TYPES OF WIRE DRAWING MACHINES

🔩
Rod Breakdown Machine (RBD)
Handles the initial large reduction from 8 mm rod down to ~1.5–2.5 mm wire. Uses high pulling forces and heavy-duty dies. Typically 9–13 draft passes in one continuous run. The first machine in any wire manufacturing line.
⚙️
Intermediate Drawing Machine
Takes wire from ~1.5 mm down to ~0.5 mm. Multiple draft blocks with individual speed control to maintain correct tension and back-tension between dies. Used for medium-sized conductor wire.
🔬
Fine Wire Drawing Machine
Produces fine wire from 0.5 mm down to 0.05 mm or smaller. Requires extremely precise die alignment, superior lubrication, and high-speed capstans. Used for signal wire, magnet wire, and fine stranded conductors.
🔀
Multi-Wire Drawing Machine
Draws multiple wires simultaneously in parallel — up to 20+ wires at once. Dramatically increases output for high-volume production. Each wire passes through its own die series with shared take-up spools.

// KEY FORMULAS

WIRE DRAWING FORMULAS

These are the essential calculations used daily in wire drawing operations. All variables are defined below the table.

ParameterFormulaDescription
Reduction in Area (RA)RA = (Dl² − Ds²) / Dl² × 100%Percentage cross-section reduced per draft
Area Reduction per DieAR = (D₁² − D₂²) / D₁²Fractional area reduction at a single die
Draw RatioDR = Dl / DsRatio of starting to finishing diameter
ElongationE = (Dl / Ds)²Length increase factor after drawing
Die Bearing LengthBl = 0.3 × Df to 0.5 × DfOptimal bearing zone length in die
True Strain (per pass)ε = 2 × ln(Dl / Ds)True plastic strain introduced per draft
Variable Definitions
Dl = Starting (larger) wire diameter  |  Ds = Finishing (smaller) wire diameter  |  Df = Finished die diameter  |  Bl = Bearing length  |  A = Die approach angle  |  N = Number of dies

// PROCESS NOTES

KEY PROCESS PARAMETERS

1
Lubrication
Proper lubrication is critical to reduce die wear, friction heat, and surface defects. Wet drawing uses oil-in-water emulsions or neat oils. Dry drawing uses soap or powder lubricants. The lubricant film must survive the extreme pressure at the die entry angle.
2
Die Angle
The semi-angle of the die approach zone (typically 6°–12°) controls the deformation zone geometry. Too shallow increases friction surface; too steep causes redundant deformation and wire breakage. Optimal angle depends on reduction ratio and material.
3
Annealing
Drawing work-hardens copper, reducing ductility. Intermediate or final annealing at 300–500°C (copper) recrystallises the grain structure, restoring elongation and flexibility. Continuous annealing lines anneal wire inline immediately after drawing.
4
Die Material
Tungsten carbide dies are standard for copper and aluminium. Polycrystalline diamond (PCD) dies are used for fine wire and hard materials — they last 10–50× longer but cost significantly more. Die wear is the primary maintenance cost in wire drawing.
5
Speed & Tension
Drawing speed ranges from 5 m/s (rod breakdown) to over 50 m/s (fine wire). Back-tension between capstans is carefully controlled — too little causes wire slip, too much risks breakage. Modern machines use closed-loop tension feedback.
