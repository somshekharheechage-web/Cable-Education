LAYING UP MACHINE

Assembling multiple individually insulated cores into the final multi-core configuration — twisted together before bedding, armour, and jacket are applied.

// OVERVIEW

WHAT IS LAYING UP?

Laying up is the process of assembling multiple insulated cores into a multi-core cable. The cores are twisted together with a defined lay length and direction, then bound with a binder tape or extruded bedding before armour and jacket are applied.

The twisted assembly reduces cable diameter compared to parallel cores, improves flexibility, and — for data cables — reduces crosstalk. The lay length and direction are tightly controlled to meet electrical performance requirements.

// PROCESS

LAYING UP PROCESS

1
Core Pay-off
Individual insulated cores are fed from supply reels. Tension must be consistent across all cores — uneven tension causes one core to stretch while others sag, producing an oval or irregular assembly and inconsistent electrical properties.
2
Forming Die
Cores pass through a closing die that brings them into the correct geometric arrangement — trefoil (3-core), quadruplex (4-core), or multi-core pattern. The die controls the outer diameter and roundness of the laid-up assembly.
3
Fillers
Gaps between round cores are filled with polypropylene yarn, PE rods, or extruded compound to achieve a circular cable form. Fillers prevent cores from shifting under bending and give the armour a stable round surface to sit on.
4
Binder Tape
A helically applied polyester, nylon, or paper tape holds the assembly together with 10–25% overlap. For water-blocking designs, a swellable tape is used — it absorbs moisture and expands to seal any water ingress path along the cable length.
5
Bedding Extrusion
Many cables receive an extruded PVC or PE bedding layer over the laid-up assembly. Bedding provides a smooth, round surface for armour application, protects cores from armour wire penetration, and adds a secondary moisture barrier.
